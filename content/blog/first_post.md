playing the word-guessing game persondothings.com 

our last tuesday night at CAMBRIA, C and i found the word-guessing game persondothings.com. in two days, we played the game for 3 hours, and argued about it for 6.  

a quick exposition on the game: it’s like charades, in that you’re trying to get someone to guess a word, and you can give hints using only a vocabulary of 33 words (words chosen to be something like basis vectors of language). 

from my point of view, the role of the hint-giver is to model the guesser: based on their guesses, figure out where their brain is, and, with that information, nudge them in the right direction. i think of words as points in a high-dimensional “vibes” space. for this reason, when we play, rather than calling it hint-giving, we say: “do you want to guess, or steer?” and to me, steering felt literally trivial - you knew where they were and you knew where you wanted them to go. 

from his point of view, the role of the hint-giver was to provide “good” hints. he thought of the game less like charades, and more like codenames, if you’re familiar with the game. his model was that words were less points in a continuous space, and more nodes of a graph, where the edges in the graph represent hints. so if someone’s guess puts them at a current word-node, it is the role of the hint-giver to find a “path” of hints to the answer. 

the [through-line] of the cumulative 6 hours of argument went something like this: 

(for context, he was trying to get me to “birthday”, when i had the words “birth” and “time” so he wanted to convert “time” → “day”)

G: “can you be more responsive? clearly if i’m stuck on ‘time’, and we both know i’m close, and i’m trying to guess things around ‘time’ but failing, telling me ‘other other other’ isn’t going to help me. you’re not giving me new information”

C: “’other other other’ is new information. by saying it over and over, i’m telling you that you’re really close, that you have all the pieces you need, and that you can just get it if you thought a bit longer. that you just need to sample the epsilon ball around ‘time’. i don’t want to introduce a costly whole new concept if you’re so close, [like how over the course of training an ml model you want to decrease your learning rate so that it can converge]” 

G: “but *empirically*, it’s not working - i just can’t think of the word. [‘sampling from a high-dimensional space is hard’.] [‘it’s a very real phenomenon that once you’re anchored, it’s hard to think in fresh directions.’] i realize that sampling the epsilon ball is somehow easy, and enjoyable, for you, but it makes me feel dumb, and i can’t believe you’re not realizing this and changing your behavior, it makes me think that some part of you *wants* to see me struggle with this” 

C: “well no i’m not trying to make it hard for you. simultaneously i’m trying to think of new hints, but it takes a while since it’s not that easy to come up with hints, just as **it isn’t easy for you to generate guesses” 

G: “ok clearly i’m insecure about my perceived inability to sample the epsilon-ball, i know that. well, i think *you’re* avoiding the **strategy of “fit a model to what i’m thinking”, which is *obviously empirically superior,* because you yourself are insecure about something *and you don’t know it*”

C: “i don’t think i’m insecure about this. i didn’t feel strongly that you were guessing much slower than me [we can track our times in a spreadsheet to see if the diff is statistically significant], so i wasn’t thinking about the difference between our strategies that much. but now that you point it out, i think yes this is something that i’d like to get better at. like a lifelong journey kind of thing”

G: “oh” 

C: “also, i think you telling yourself that you’re anchored and therefore screwed is not helping you. i think you are allowing yourself to be bad at sampling the epsilon ball partly because you want to be right about that fact”

G: “oh, you’re right”
